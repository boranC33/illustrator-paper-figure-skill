# Mechanism Figure Style Rubric

Load this before final approval of a mechanism/process figure, or when revising a figure that looks crude despite being technically correct.

Score each category from 0 to 2. A manuscript-ready mechanism figure should usually score at least 10/14, with no category at 0.

## 1. Visual Hierarchy

- 0: All objects have equal visual weight; the reader cannot tell what matters first.
- 1: Primary object is identifiable, but arrows/labels compete with it.
- 2: Primary mechanism is visually dominant, secondary elements support it, labels are calm.

## 2. Domain-Specific Geometry

- 0: Generic boxes, circles, and lines stand in for real components.
- 1: Main components are recognizable but simplified.
- 2: Important components include credible proportions, internal features, cutaway surfaces, or repeated details.

## 3. Line Quality

- 0: Single line weight everywhere or inconsistent accidental weights.
- 1: Basic line hierarchy exists.
- 2: Outer contours, internal details, hatching, callouts, and arrows have deliberate, consistent weights.

## 4. Material And State Cues

- 0: No distinction between metal, fluid, heat, gas, tissue, interface, or computational layers.
- 1: Color distinguishes states but lacks texture/detail.
- 2: Materials/states use restrained color plus outlines, hatching, transparency, bands, or small details.

## 5. Color And Tone

- 0: Colors are saturated, decorative, or one-note.
- 1: Palette is restrained but slightly flat.
- 2: Palette has neutral structure plus 2-4 semantic accents and remains readable in grayscale.

## 6. Detail Density

- 0: Too sparse or too cluttered.
- 1: Detail exists but is unevenly distributed.
- 2: Details support interpretation and are concentrated around the mechanism, with quiet surrounding space.

## 7. Publication Polish

- 0: Looks like a draft or default diagram.
- 1: Clean and readable, but not visually memorable.
- 2: Feels intentionally designed, editable, and consistent with high-impact manuscript figures.

## Revision Moves

- Add structural details before adding decoration.
- Use line-weight hierarchy before adding color.
- Add cutaway walls, hatching, highlights, or small repeated features when components look flat.
- Reduce arrow size/contrast when arrows overpower the mechanism.
- Replace generic blocks with domain-specific silhouettes or semi-realistic vector parts.
- Harmonize colors into neutral base, state accents, and muted labels.
- Use an art direction contract before redrawing from scratch.
